# Ben Cao Gang Mu — Frequently Asked Questions

## Q1: Who was Li Shizhen and why is his work important?
A: Li Shizhen (1518–1593) was a Chinese physician who spent 27 years researching and writing the *Ben Cao Gang Mu*, consulting over 800 source texts and personally traveling to verify information. It's considered the greatest pharmacological compendium in Chinese history and was recognized by UNESCO as a Memory of the World in 2011 for its global scientific significance.

## Q2: How is the *Ben Cao Gang Mu* organized?
A: The text contains 1,892 entries organized into 16 major divisions (water, fire, earth, metals/stones, herbs, grains, vegetables, fruits, trees, clothing/tools, insects, scaled animals, shell animals, birds, quadrupeds, and humans), subdivided into 60 orders. Each entry includes alternative names, corrections of prior errors, energetic properties (qi and flavor), therapeutic applications, Li's personal observations, and formulas.

## Q3: What does "qi and flavor" mean in the text?
A: The Four Qi classify herbs by temperature (cold, cool, neutral, warm, hot) to treat corresponding conditions. The Five Flavors (sour, bitter, sweet, pungent, salty) indicate organ affinity and primary action—for example, sweet tonifies and bitter dries dampness.

## Q4: How did Li Shizhen correct prior medical errors?
A: Li systematically identified misidentifications where different texts used the same name for different plants, distinguished regional name variations, and corrected false claims through personal investigation—tasting herbs, traveling to examine specimens, and interviewing herbalists and farmers.

## Q5: Which herbs from the *Ben Cao Gang Mu* have modern scientific validation?
A: Artemisia annua (artemisinin, 2015 Nobel Prize), ephedra (ephedrine for respiration), rhubarb (anthraquinones), and coptis (berberine for metabolic disorders) are among the most researched. Modern pharmacologists have validated dozens of Li's observations.

## Q6: How quickly did the work spread globally?
A: Within decades, it reached Japan (1607), Korea, and Vietnam. European naturalists were astounded by its scope—Charles Darwin referenced it, and Joseph Needham studied it extensively in his *Science and Civilisation in China*.
