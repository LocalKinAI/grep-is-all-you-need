# Ben Cao Gang Mu (Compendium of Materia Medica)
## 本草綱目

**Author:** Li Shizhen (李時珍, 1518–1593 CE)
**Compiled:** 1552–1578 CE (27 years of research)
**First printed:** 1596 CE, Nanjing (three years after Li's death)
**Source:** Wikipedia (CC BY-SA), UNESCO Memory of the World, UC Press academic resources (public domain content)
**Scale:** 53 volumes, ~1.9 million Chinese characters, 1,892 entries, 1,160 illustrations, 11,096 formulas

---

## Historical Context

The *Ben Cao Gang Mu* is the greatest pharmacological compendium in Chinese history and one of the most comprehensive works of natural history produced anywhere before the scientific revolution. Li Shizhen spent 27 years writing it, consulting over **800 source texts** and personally traveling to examine specimens, interview herbalists, miners, farmers, and fishermen.

Li Shizhen was born into a physician family in Qizhou (modern Hubei province). Though he failed the imperial examinations three times, he served as a physician at the Imperial Academy of Medicine in Beijing, gaining access to the imperial library's vast collection. He left after three years to complete his life's work.

The text was presented to the Wanli Emperor, who paid little attention to it. Li died in 1593, three years before its first printing. His son Li Jianyuan saw it through publication. Within decades it had spread across Asia and been translated into multiple languages, eventually becoming known to European naturalists who were astounded by its scope.

**UNESCO Memory of the World:** The *Ben Cao Gang Mu* was inscribed in 2011 as part of the UNESCO Memory of the World Register, recognizing its global cultural and scientific significance.

## Structure and Organization

### The 16 Divisions (部, bù)

Li Shizhen organized all substances into 16 major divisions, subdivided into 60 orders (類, lèi):

| Division | Chinese | Contents |
|----------|---------|---------|
| **Water** | 水部 | Natural waters — rain, dew, springs, rivers, wells |
| **Fire** | 火部 | Forms of fire and heat — flames, embers, charcoal |
| **Earth** | 土部 | Soils, clays, minerals from earth |
| **Metal & Stone** | 金石部 | Metals, ores, gems, minerals |
| **Herbs (Mountain)** | 草部 | Mountain and wilderness herbs |
| **Grains** | 穀部 | Cereal grains and legumes |
| **Vegetables** | 菜部 | Culinary and medicinal vegetables |
| **Fruits** | 果部 | Fruits and nuts |
| **Trees** | 木部 | Trees and woody plants |
| **Clothing & Tools** | 服器部 | Garments, textiles, utensils |
| **Insects** | 蟲部 | Insects and invertebrates |
| **Scaled Animals** | 鱗部 | Fish, reptiles, and scaled creatures |
| **Shell Animals** | 介部 | Shellfish, turtles, crustaceans |
| **Birds** | 禽部 | Birds and poultry |
| **Quadrupeds** | 獸部 | Mammals — wild and domestic |
| **Humans** | 人部 | Human-derived medicinal substances |

### Each Entry Contains:

1. **Release Time (釋名)** — alternative names, synonyms, etymology
2. **Correction of Errors (集解)** — Li's commentary correcting prior misidentifications
3. **Qi and Flavor (氣味)** — energetic properties: temperature (cold/cool/neutral/warm/hot) and flavor (sour/bitter/sweet/pungent/salty)
4. **Main Indications (主治)** — classical therapeutic applications from prior texts
5. **Li's Own Discoveries (發明)** — Li Shizhen's personal clinical observations and experiments
6. **Attached Formulas (附方)** — compound prescriptions using the substance

## Key Entries: Major Medicinal Substances

### Premier Tonics

**Ren Shen (人參 — Ginseng, Panax ginseng)**
- Temperature: slightly warm; Flavor: sweet, slightly bitter
- Functions: Powerfully tonifies yuan qi (source qi), supplements spleen and lung, generates fluids, calms the spirit
- Li's observation: "Among all herbs that supplement the five organs and calm the spirit, none surpasses ginseng"
- Key formulas: Si Jun Zi Tang (Four Gentlemen Decoction), Du Shen Tang (Single Ginseng Decoction) for extreme qi collapse
- Caution: Contraindicated with acute infection (excess heat conditions)

**Huang Qi (黃耙 — Astragalus, Astragalus membranaceus)**
- Temperature: slightly warm; Flavor: sweet
- Functions: Tonifies defensive qi (wei qi), raises sunken qi, promotes wound healing, stops spontaneous sweating
- Li's application: Used extensively for prolapse conditions and non-healing wounds
- Modern validation: Beta-glucans and polysaccharides confirmed as immunomodulatory

**Dang Gui (當歸 — Dong Quai, Angelica sinensis)**
- Temperature: warm; Flavor: sweet, pungent
- Functions: Nourishes and invigorates blood, regulates menstruation, moistens dryness
- "The primary herb for all blood disorders in women"
- The name means "ought to return" — returning the blood to its proper flow
